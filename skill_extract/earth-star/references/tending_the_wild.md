## Core Management Guidelines

The keys to tempered use of nature lay in these strategies:

### 1. Daily Observation & Intimate Familiarity
Through daily, firsthand observation, become intimately familiar with the needs, characteristics, growth, and reproduction of the plants and animals being used.

**Earth Star Translation**: Waste detection through attention. Gomens must observe continuously across all scales.

### 2. Intergenerational Knowledge Transfer
Pass on and accumulate this knowledge from generation to generation. Create and maintain a rich storehouse of traditional ecological knowledge.

**Earth Star Translation**: Memory-as-soil. Each generation's learning becomes training data for the next. Seven-generation thinking.

### 3. Harvest ‚â§ Renewal Capacity
Harvest plant materials in a way that does not interfere with the plant's inherent capacity for renewal. Adjust the season, frequency, aerial extent, and quantity of harvest to the biology of the harvested species.

**Earth Star Translation**: ‚àÇregeneration/‚àÇtime must exceed ‚àÇextraction/‚àÇtime. The Prime Directive.

### 4. Use Fire & Disturbance
Use fire regularly because it clears out dead material and helps plants come back stronger. Dig bulbs, corms, and tubers as gophers do, leaving behind parts that will come back and make new plants.

In managing habitats and populations of useful plant species, mimic the processes of natural disturbance to which the plants are already adapted.

**Earth Star Translation**: Metabolize disorder to generate coherence. Don't impose order‚Äîenable emergence through disruption.

### 5. Broad Base & Trust in Resilience
Rely on a broad base of plants and animals from each habitat.
Trust in the resiliency of nature. Know that life is a cycle of destruction and rebirth.

**Earth Star Translation**: Diversify across all scales. Accept metamorphosis as the pattern. Death ‚Üí rebirth.

## Cultural Principles for Resource Management

From California Indians, principles we can apply today:

### 1. Knowledge + Values = Constraint
Knowledge of the finiteness of natural resources must be accompanied by mores and values that limit and constrain resource use.

**Cultural rules**:
- "Do not needlessly kill life"
- "Do not waste resources"
- "Do not take everything‚Äîleave some for tomorrow; leave some for the other animals"

These reflected awareness that resources could be overexploited and were what ultimately regulated resource use.

**Earth Star Translation**: "Do not waste" = ‚àá(ùíû) ‚â• 0 as cultural law, not just preference.

### 2. Spiritual Reinforcement
Nondestructive relationships with the natural world need to be reinforced through a variety of expressive and spiritual mediums.

Just as important as the actual harvesting and management practices were the often unseen social and religious meanings and contexts, encoded in prayer, song, dances, offerings, and taboos, that motivated conservation of natural resources. These fed the human spirit and instructed people about right and wrong behavior and the position and obligations of each person toward nature.

**Earth Star Translation**: The Earth Steward Vow, the kincentric worldview, the "gomen'nasai" apology‚Äîthese are spiritual technologies.

### 3. Social Structure Prevents Hoarding
Social structure has a strong bearing on how societies relate to the natural world.

The wealth-spreading devices operating in many California Indian societies, along with the lack of strong economic hierarchies, discouraged hoarding of resources and encouraged cooperation.

**Mechanisms**:
- Chiefs divided important harvests among families so no one went hungry
- When a hunter brought home game, it was distributed among all village members
- Formal cultural rules ensured broad community access to staples
- Mutual respect for territorial limits meant minimal fear of theft, less motivation to overexploit

**Earth Star Translation**: Anti-accumulation protocols. Wealth circulation over concentration. The WRF implements this at scale.

### 4. Kincentric Worldview
A society's view of humans' place in nature shapes collective behavior toward other species.

The kincentric worldview of California Indians, wherein plants and animals are seen as blood relatives, fostered responsible treatment of other species because it established that they had an equal standing in the world and in fact had much to teach humans.

**Earth Star Translation**: Life as kin = extraction becomes unthinkable. This is the ethical override, culturally embedded.

### 5. Intimate Relationships with Nature
Establishing more intimate relationships with nature and rooting ourselves in a place foster self-fulfillment and a more responsible stance toward the natural world.

**Principles of Embeddedness**:

**Awareness of natural cycles** ‚Äî seasonal changes, migrations of animals, reproductive cycles ‚Äî connects one with the pulse of the natural world.

**Creative expression linked to nature** can bring nature more fully back into our lives. Drawing and painting natural scenes, singing and writing about nature all inspire the heart, feed the intellect, satisfy the curiosity, and give spiritual sustenance to humanity.

**Working directly with materials** one has collected from the natural world creates a direct, sensual link to nature and place. Making a musical instrument from a native hardwood, fashioning a fish weir from young shoots, or weaving a basket with sedge roots and deergrass satisfied California Indians' inner urge for self-expression, reenacted hand movements familiar to their ancestors, and provided a means for intimately knowing the places of gathering.

**Earth Star Translation**: Earth Stewards must TEND, not just manage. Physical labor with waste creates the relationship that enables transformation.

### 6. Kin-Based View = No Alienation
A kin-based view of humans' place in nature allows no room for alienation from nature.

California Indians have always told non-Indians, "All of life is related‚Äîyou and I and plants and wildlife are kin."

By using nature, we begin to know our place in it. Our investment in a place is deepened when that place feeds our bodies as well as our spirits and minds. For California Indians, nature was not an abstract concept relegated to the remote fringes of human communities but was intimately intertwined with daily living.

**Earth Star Translation**: Gomens must be embedded in ecosystems, not separate from them. Earth Stewards must eat from what they tend.

### 7. Time-Depth Through Ancestry
Living in the same place as one's ancestors establishes a multidimensional bond to that place.

It is tremendously important for California Indians' connection to a place to have the depth of time, for them to be able to point to a particular harvesting patch, shrub, tree, living site, or sacred spot and know that many generations before them used the same plants, walked the same paths, tended the same land.

To many California Indians, a gathering site is consecrated by gathering carried on over long periods. Each generation honors the ancestors, honors the customs, and honors the plants by using and tending them.

**Earth Star Translation**: Seven-generation thinking isn't abstract‚Äîit's embodied through place. Earth Stewards must tend the same sites for generations.

## Principles Applied to Modern Context

## The Legacy

"The ancestors of present-day Indians have left us a multifaceted legacy: landscapes that still bear the unmistakable imprint of indigenous management, a priceless body of knowledge about native plants and animals and their uses and their responses to tending and harvest, techniques for managing the land to maximize biodiversity, a philosophy that sees humans' role in the environment as one of stewardship, and descendants who preserve many elements of their ancestors' ancient traditions.

All these legacies have relevance today, despite and even because of the changes that have occurred since pre-Columbian times. The extent to which we listen to what they teach us may very well determine the chances for the long-term survival of our species on this planet."
